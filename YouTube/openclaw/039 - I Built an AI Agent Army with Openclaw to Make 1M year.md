## Summary

Doobie demonstrates an autonomous "app factory" that runs 11 AI agents 24/7 under a single OpenClaw orchestrator (Sheldon) to research, build, monetize, test, package, and prepare iOS apps for submission with minimal human intervention. The system produced seven apps in one afternoon and is designed as a volume-and-iteration pipeline rather than trying to make one perfect product: *All apps go through a ninestep rigorous framework.* He frames the opportunity with app-market stats and examples (one niche app made $90,000 in a month; a snoring app reportedly makes $400,000/month) to justify shipping many lightweight apps and doubling down on winners.

The architecture is hierarchical: Sheldon routes tasks to specialized subagents (project leads) so each agent keeps a small context footprint and the orchestrator never hits context bloat. Four models are used across 11 agents: Claude Opus 4.6 builds Swift/SwiftUI code, Codex 5.3 reviews code and runs crash/permission checks, Sonet 4.6 handles routing/logging and quick decisions, and additional models/templates supply payments/onboarding/AI feature wrappers. Apps pass six automated quality gates, must score ≥8/10 to proceed (three failures flag human review), and get payments wired via Apple StoreKit with monetization chosen from the validated research (free trial vs premium). The factory also auto-generates app store listings, icons, screenshots (by driving the UI to capture shots), and promo videos, leaving only the final submit approval to the developer.

Marketing is treated as part two of the system and is allotted most of the agent context: the plan is autonomous native-content channels (TikTok/Instagram) run by separate agents to build niche audiences and funnel installs when relevant apps ship. Research agents scan Reddit and X for pain points and app-store gaps, then the validation, build, QA, monetization, and promo-video steps follow automatically so a new app can be announced to ready audiences almost instantly. Doobie emphasizes iteration and automated learning loops (eg, retry/adjust cycles on failures and content hooks that self-optimize), closing with the idea that *"The best automation is the kind you forget you built."*
